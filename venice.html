Cannaregio: Traditionally working-class and now a blend of locals and students. Cannaregio is the most populous sestiere, home to the historic Jewish Ghettothe first of its kind. It has a quiet charm, with tranquil canals, artisan workshops, and casual bacari (wine bars).

Castello: Rooted in naval power and everyday life. Once the center of Venices shipbuilding might, Castello still holds echoes of the Arsenales glory. It stretches from crowded tourist paths near San Marco to sleepy corners where laundry flaps in the breeze.

San Polo: It has been a commercial hub since the early days of the Republic. The Rialto Bridge and Market testify to its mercantile past. Its compact, energetic, and rich with medieval charm and some of the best food and wine in the city.

San Marco: The political and religious heart of the Venetian Republic. Grand and opulent, San Marco is where Venice displayed its power. The sestiere radiates wealth and history, with St. Marks Basilica, the Doges Palace, and luxury cafs.

Santa Croce: The least visited, and most functional. Historically home to salt warehouses and traders, it retains a gritty, utilitarian feel. There are quiet corners like San Giacomo dellOrio, but this sestiere also handles Venices infrastructure, with Piazzale Roma and the bus terminal.

Dorsoduro: Bohemian and elegant. Historically an area for scholars and artists, its home to major art collections like the Gallerie dellAccademia and Peggy Guggenheim. The vibe is relaxed, with sun-soaked quays, wide canals, and a youthful energy near Ca Foscari University.

VENICE
There were 5,876,797 arrivals to Venice in 2024.
There were 13,291,000 stays* in Venice in 2024.

* 1 stay = 1 night spent

Apart from a significant drop in 2020 due to the Coronavirus pandemic, tourism to the floating city has been growing consistently. People are attracted here from the whole world to marvel at the unique aesthetic of a city immersed in a lagoon, where you move by boat instead of by car, where each secret square - dispersed among a maze of narrow streets - is an artistic and architectonic gem, and where you can soak in all this while enjoying some of Italys best menu items - pizza, Spritz, ice cream, seafood,

The city has had to adapt to the influx of tourists, by becoming one of the densest hotel locations in Italy. Alongside the classic tourist accommodations, such as Bed and Breakfasts, Hotels, Hostels, etc., private structures have started to spring up to respond to tourists rising demand for beds.

Platforms like AirBnB, on the uptake globally, have boomed in Venice and even though they don't outnumber classic hotel structures, they were proportionally less impacted by Covid and are exponentially increasing.

So how can there be more AirBnBs than hotels? Due to their higher capacity, hotels host more arrivals but are actually fewer in number, as you can see by zooming into this map, where a shows Airbnb listings and a shows a hotel (and its name). According to many local residents, the situation has gotten out of hand. Venice has become one of the most visited cities in Europe per square km.

As mentioned, last year, there were 5 million visitors - far exceeding the local population of just 50,000.

In a single day, on average, there were around 13,000 tourists in Venice in 2024. That's about 25% of all individuals in Venice on any day, with the local population making up the remaining 75%.

Moreover, the presence of tourists and especially the AirBnB infrastructure has made rent prices skyrocket. Even accounting for overall inflation in the last years, the city has become very expensive for locals.

The unsustainable situation in Venice, where prices, mass tourism and waste have turned the city into an over-crowded theme park, has created the premise for many residents to leave. In the last 15 years, the greater Venice urban area has lots 20,000 residents, with over half of them leaving from the small but hyper-touristic historical city centre (the area shown in the maps of this project).

This will inevitably change the face of Venice, which will never be the same without its local charme.

In view of this and of rising sea levels, which are eating away at the foundations of the lagoon city every day, the city of Venice has put in place a tax to control the arrivals of tourists. On top of a tax paid at hotels on each stay, tourists have to pay a 5 euro daily entrance tax to the city. This is done to disincentivise one-day visits, which are partly responsible for overcrowding the city. Venice has also joined the WWF network of Plastic Smart Cities to reduce single plastic use and waste management. Another issue that should be prioritised are the cruise ships, which have damaged the coastal habitats as well as the foundations of the city, and that according to some residents may be to blame for the extreme 2019 floods that hit the city.
What is sure, is that tourism in Venice has to be re-thought systemically, in order to ensure that the city remains livable for its residents and available for tourists to visit sustainably in the future.
